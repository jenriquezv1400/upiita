Theory
Examples
1. direct speech: 'Do you work from home?' he said.
a. indirect speech: He asked me if I worked from home.
2. direct speech: 'Who did you see?' she asked.
a. indirect speech: She asked me who I'd seen.
3. direct speech: 'Could you write that down for me?' she asked.
a. indirect speech: She asked me to write it down.
Grammar explanation
A reported question is when we tell someone what another person asked. To do this, we can
use direct speech or indirect speech.
1. direct speech: 'Do you like working in sales?' he asked.
a. indirect speech: He asked me if I liked working in sales.
In indirect speech, we change the question structure (e.g. Do you like) to a statement
structure (e.g. I like).
We also often make changes to the tenses and other words in the same way as for reported
statements (e.g. have done had done, today that day). You can learn about these changes
on the Reported speech 1 statements page.
Yes/no questions
In yes/no questions, we use if or whether to report the question. If is more common.
1. 'Are you going to the Helsinki conference?'
a. He asked me if I was going to the Helsinki conference.
2. 'Have you nished the project yet?'
a. She asked us whether we'd nished the project yet.
3
Questions with a question word
In what, where, why, who, when or how questions, we use the question word to report the
question.
1. 'What time does the train leave?'
a. He asked me what time the train left.
2. 'Where did he go?'
a. She asked where he went.
Reporting verbs
The most common reporting verb for questions is ask, but we can also use verbs like enquire,
want to know or wonder.
1. 'Did you bring your passports?'
a. She wanted to know if they'd brought their passports.
2. 'When could you get this done by?'
a. He wondered when we could get it done by.
Offers, requests and suggestions
If the question is making an offer, request or suggestion, we can use a specic verb pattern
instead, for example offer + innitive, ask + innitive or suggest + ing.
1. 'Would you like me to help you?'
a. He offered to help me.
2. 'Can you hold this for me, please?'
a. She asked me to hold it.
3. 'Why don't we check with Joel?'
a. She suggested checking with Joel.
